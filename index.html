Situated Modelling

Situated Modelling aims at fostering a reflexive and co-laborative approach to modelling human-environment relations. The term is inspired by Donna Haraway’s (1988) notion of situated knowledges. Situated Modelling is a way of researching and reflecting on modelling practices, which ultimately and in co-laboration might also lead to modelling differently.

We are an interdisciplinary group of researchers who study modelling practices from a Science and Technology Studies (STS), anthropological, human geographical and philosophical perspective and/or use (numerical) modelling methods in their research. We engage with different modelling approaches on different scales: agent-based models in social-ecological systems research, hydrological models, earth system models, system dynamics and causal loop modelling and participatory approaches. We jointly organize workshops and summer schools, teach in graduate student seminars, write articles and learn across disciplinary boundaries.

Our story started at IRI THESys, Humboldt-Universität zu Berlin. Today, even as we explore new directions, we remain a community that works and learns together.

From situated knowledges to situated modelling

Situated Modelling extends a relational understanding of the world to modelling to enable a systematic interrogation of modelling choices and their consequences. We analyse the situatedness of three modelling approaches and suggest four guiding principles for Situated Modelling.

Enactments of knowledge and social order in participatory modelling

An ethnographic story about participatory modelling in sustainability research which suggests that science might relate to social order, and therefore democracy, because research practices enact specific versions of the ‘multiple objects’ world, knowledge, science, people, and ethics.

Making a case for power-sensitive water modelling

The power embedded in models is often concealed by claiming their neutrality. An emerging body of work offers critical insights into the political implications of hydrological models and a nuanced understanding of their application in context.

Situating Bayesian knowledge

We explore the contingency and performativity of knowing in Bayesian ways by revisiting a case of pollutant transfers from land to water. We query this material from an STS perspective and discuss how Bayesian modelling practices enact aspects of the world and can (re)configure (social) relations.

Situating hydrological modeling

We explore how the power of models can be explicitly and constructively engaged with, thereby expanding their potential to support transformations to water justice and sustainability.

Modelling water worlds

We present three complementary entry points for analysing models and modelling practices. Models not only contribute to reproducing water inequalities but that they can also be mobilised to understand and address them.
